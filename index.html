About Our Laboratory

We are interested in combining experimental and computational approaches for drug design, especially targeting neglected and emerging diseases. Most projects in our group involve structure-based drug design to discover and optimize bioactive compounds. Also, considering potential applications for medicinal chemistry, we are interested in characterizing new protein targets or druggable pockets, and in developing new tools. Our main research topics are:

Structure-based drug design (mostly) for neglected and emerging diseases

Aware of the need to develop better drugs to treat neglected and emerging diseases, and considering how much rational drug design can aid in this process, we employ techniques such as experimental screening, virtual screening, molecular docking, and molecular dynamics simulations to discover and optimize potential drug candidates. We have been focused mostly on proteases such as cruzain1–3, a target for Chagas disease, the NS2B/NS3 Zika virus protease, and SARS-CoV2 Mpro (a target for COVID-19)4,5. Ongoing research also includes parasite proteasomes as targets for malaria and Chagas disease, SRPKs as targets for cancer treatment and dihydrofolate reductase for tuberculosis treatment. Among varied strategies to discover inhibitors for these targets, we are currently very interested in exploring ultra large chemical spaces through virtual screening, always followed by experimental validation6.

While most of our projects focus on small molecules, we also study protein-protein interactions, aiming to investigate the biological role of specific proteins and the key elements for their interaction, understand selectivity, and design bioactive peptides.

Computational and biochemical characterization of therapeutic targets

Given the need to further validate potential therapeutic targets, we have contributed to the characterization of protease targets. In the case of Zika virus protease, we have characterized a new allosteric site experimentally and through MD simulations7. Using constant pH simulations, we also studied how the protonation states of active site residues vary depending on the pH and in the presence of a ligand4, to guide structure-based drug design studies.

While there has been much effort to develop cruzain inhibitors as therapeutics for Chagas disease, there are still many open questions concerning the multigenic family of cruzipains. We recently determined the genomic organization of this family in three Trypanosoma cruzi, and divided their members into four cruzipain subtypes. Cruzain is the recombinant form of cruzipain 1, the only subtype targeted in medicinal chemistry efforts. However, we detected the expression of the several cruzipain subtypes throughout the T. cruzi life cycle and observed important differences in the active site residues among the four subtypes8,9. Our current efforts aim to understand the biological roles of each cruzipain subtype and their relevance for drug design.

Virtual Screening

Structure-Based Virtual Screening Pipelines

We develop and apply structure-based virtual screening workflows to prospect large molecular libraries against pharmacologically relevant targets. Our pipelines integrate molecular docking engines (GNINA, AutoDock Vina) with machine learning enrichment strategies — including deep learning approaches such as DeepDocking — enabling systematic exploration of multi-million compound libraries with high computational efficiency on HPC environments.

Kinase Biology

Kinase Allostery & Selective Inhibitor Design

We investigate allosteric and cryptic binding sites in kinases — with a particular focus on the SRPK family — characterizing conformational dynamics, DFG-in/out equilibria, and non-catalytic regions such as the docking groove and spacer insert domain. This work provides structural rationale for selective inhibitors that go beyond the ATP-competitive site and modulate kinase function through alternative mechanisms.

Molecular Dynamics

Molecular Dynamics & Free Energy Methods

We apply molecular dynamics simulations and free energy calculation methods (MM-PBSA, FEP) to characterize protein–ligand binding thermodynamics, conformational ensembles, and transition pathways between distinct states. These approaches are central to uncovering transiently accessible binding pockets, rationalizing observed selectivity profiles, and prioritizing hits from virtual screening campaigns for further optimization.

Principal Investigator
Prof. Dr. Rafaela Salgado Ferreira
She holds a B.Pharm. degree from the Federal University of Minas Gerais (UFMG) and a Ph.D. in Chemistry and Chemical Biology from the University of California, San Francisco (UCSF, USA). She completed postdoctoral training at the University of São Paulo (USP) in 2010 and served as a visiting researcher at the Centre de Biochimie Structurale (Montpellier, France) in 2018. Since 2011, she has been a faculty member of the Department of Biochemistry and Immunology at UFMG and serves as coordinator of the Molecular Modeling and Drug Design Laboratory, where she employs computational and experimental approaches for the discovery and development of novel ligands targeting therapeutically relevant proteins. Her research focuses on rational drug design, with expertise in virtual screening, molecular docking, comparative modeling, and structural biology. She served as Head of the Department of Biochemistry and Immunology at the Institute of Biological Sciences, UFMG (2024–2026), and currently serves as Treasurer of the Medicinal Chemistry Division of the Brazilian Chemical Society (SBQ) for the 2024–2026 term. She is an affiliated member of the Center for Discovery and Innovation in Parasitic Diseases (CDIPD) at the University of California San Diego (USA) and an Affiliated Member of the Brazilian Academy of Sciences (2025–2029). Her contributions have been recognized through several prestigious awards, including the L'Oréal–UNESCO–Brazilian Academy of Sciences (ABC) For Women in Science Award (Chemistry Category, 2017), the L'Oréal–UNESCO International Rising Talent Award (2018), the SBQ–ACS Brazilian Women in Chemistry Award (Emerging Leader Category, 2021), and the Early Career Researcher Award (2022), presented by the Medicinal Chemistry Division of the Brazilian Chemical Society and the Organizing Committee of the 10th Brazilian Symposium on Medicinal Chemistry (BrazMedChem). She is a Research Productivity Fellow of the Brazilian National Council for Scientific and Technological Development (CNPq), Level B, in the Biophysics Committee.

Master's Student
Vinícius Delgado
His project aims to identify novel inhibitors of the Schistosoma mansoni p38 kinase (Smp38). Schistosomiasis is a neglected tropical disease with a high prevalence in Brazil and across Africa. The emergence of resistance to praziquantel, the current standard treatment, together with the lack of effective therapeutic options against the early stages of infection, highlights the urgent need for new treatment strategies. Building upon ligands that have previously demonstrated promising in vitro activity against Smp38 (Smp38_3, Smp38_7, and Smp38_8), the study employs a comprehensive in silico drug discovery workflow. This approach integrates structural modeling of the enzyme using Modeller and AlphaFold2, molecular docking studies performed with DOCK6 and DockThor, and molecular dynamics simulations using GROMACS to identify the essential pharmacophoric features required for target binding. Based on these findings, a virtual library of synthetically accessible analogs will be designed and screened. Finally, the most promising computationally predicted compounds will be selected for organic synthesis and subsequent in vitro biological validation, with the ultimate goal of advancing the development of new therapeutic candidates against schistosomiasis.
